# The night train window

When Mara took the late train home, she chose the seat by the window because it turned the whole trip into a moving strip of theater. Warehouses slid past in blocks of sodium orange. Empty platforms flashed by like unfinished thoughts. Every few minutes the glass caught her reflection and laid it on top of the city as if she were haunting her own commute.

She liked that the train never asked her to hurry. It rattled, sighed, and accepted each station in order. By the time it reached the river bridge, the day had softened into something she could carry without clenching around it. The lights on the water looked less like evidence and more like permission to rest.
